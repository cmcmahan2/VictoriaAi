Polymarket 5-Minute BTC Markets — Deep Research (Post-Fee Era) Compiled July 7, 2026. Scope restricted to sources dated after the January 2026 fee change, except where pre-2026 background is explicitly flagged. All numbers should be re-verified against live API values before deploying capital — Polymarket has changed fee parameters at least twice in 2026 without announcement. This is research, not financial advice. Executive Summary The referee for the 5-minute Bitcoin Up/Down markets is the Chainlink BTC/USD Data Stream — an aggregated, sub-second oracle price, not any single exchange's spot price. The market rules state this explicitly and warn the market settles on Chainlink's number, "not according to other sources or spot markets." The single most actionable finding: Polymarket exposes the exact oracle feed it resolves against through a free, unauthenticated WebSocket (the Real-Time Data Socket), and community bot-builders have confirmed the stream matches the on-page "Price to Beat" to the millisecond at the window boundary. Your bot can literally watch the same number the referee uses. The "7% fee" is the crypto taker fee rate (feeRate = 0.07) in Polymarket's fee formula, quietly enabled on short-duration crypto markets on January 6, 2026. It charges takers 0.07 × p × (1−p) per share, which works out to a maximum of ~1.75¢ per share at 50¢, and — because of how the curve interacts with position economics — roughly 7% of deployed capital on cheap longshot shares and roughly 7% of gross upside on expensive near-certain shares. That symmetry is what gutted the two dominant zero-fee strategies: coin-flip-range HFT scalping and last-second sniping of nearly-decided windows. The community consensus at the time, and the pattern in every post-mortem since, is that the fee was aimed squarely at high-frequency taker bots and wash-volume farming. The surviving profitable bot population has migrated overwhelmingly to maker-side execution (limit orders pay zero fees and earn rebates), and Polymarket has since layered on two rebate systems — a Maker Rebates Program (20% of crypto taker fees redistributed daily to filled makers) and, as of May 28, 2026, a tiered Taker Rebate Program that refunds 12–50% of taker fees to high-volume accounts, with crypto volume weighted 2.3× for tier progression. 1. The Referee: What Price Resolves These Markets 1.1 The official resolution source Each 5-minute market ("Bitcoin Up or Down — [date], [time range] ET") resolves Up if the Chainlink BTC/USD price at the end of the window is greater than or equal to the price at the start , and Down otherwise. Note the tie-breaker: an exactly flat window resolves Up. The resolution source named in the rules is Chainlink's BTC/USD Data Stream, published at data.chain.link/streams/btc-usd, and the rules explicitly disclaim all other sources and spot markets. (Source: live Polymarket 5-min BTC market pages, Feb–Jul 2026.) This is not Binance's price, not Coinbase's price, and not any single venue . Chainlink Data Streams is Chainlink's low-latency oracle product: sub-second, cryptographically signed price reports aggregated across multiple exchanges and data providers, with features like mark prices and liquidity-weighted bid/ask designed to resist manipulation of any one venue. Polymarket paired Data Streams with Chainlink Automation (which executes the on-chain settlement at the predetermined window timestamps) in an integration announced September 12, 2025 — that's the pre-2026 background that makes the whole product possible. A developer write-up from April 2026 describes the settlement flow: the contract reads the Chainlink feed at the window end, compares against the Price to Beat, and settles with no human involvement. 1.2 How to watch the exact feed the referee uses (the key engineering fact) Polymarket runs a public Real-Time Data Socket (RTDS) at wss://ws-live-data.polymarket.com with no authentication required. It carries two crypto price topics: crypto_prices_chainlink — the Chainlink oracle price, symbols in slash format (filter: btc/usd). This is the resolution feed. A bot-builder in a widely-shared GitHub discussion (June 2026) confirmed that subscribing to this topic and capturing the first tick at or after the window-boundary timestamp reproduces the market's "Price to Beat" exactly, at +0ms offset from the boundary. That is the open/strike price for the window, and the same logic at window close is the settlement price. crypto_prices — a Binance-sourced price (symbols like btcusdt). Useful as the faster leading indicator, but it is not what settles the market. Three supplementary access paths worth knowing: (a) Polymarket's docs offer a sponsored Chainlink API key with onboarding support (a signup form linked from the RTDS documentation page) for traders who want direct Data Streams access with HMAC authentication rather than going through Polymarket's relay; (b) the Chainlink BTC/USD feed contract exists on Polygon (address beginning 0xc907E116...), but community reports say decoding round IDs for specific timestamps is painful and not worth it versus the WebSocket; (c) market discovery is deterministic — 5-minute windows sit on Unix timestamps divisible by 300, with slugs of the form btc-updown-5m-{window_ts}, so a bot computes the active market from the clock rather than searching for it. There is also a price-to-beat REST endpoint pattern documented for Polymarket's equity up/down markets (polymarket.com/api/equity/price-to-beat/{slug}); some 5-min bot projects instead scrape the Price to Beat off the page with Playwright, which the WebSocket method makes unnecessary. 1.3 The Binance–Chainlink gap Because most bots signal off Binance (1-second klines are the standard input) while settlement happens on the Chainlink aggregate, the basis between the two feeds is both a documented failure mode and a potential edge. Public bot projects explicitly flag "bot uses Binance data; Polymarket resolves via Chainlink" as a known risk. One April 2026 builder logged BTC from Binance, Coinbase, and Chainlink simultaneously specifically to study how divergence affected order book depth and positioning. One analysis of 2025–2026 data estimated that roughly 15–20% of 5-minute windows are decided by movement in the final ~10 seconds — precisely when a small Binance-vs-Chainlink divergence can flip the outcome relative to what a Binance-only bot believes. Practical implication: log both RTDS topics tick-by-tick, compute the rolling basis, and measure how often the sign of the window outcome differs between the two feeds in the last 10–30 seconds. That number is the ceiling on how much a Binance-signaled, late-entry strategy can trust itself. 2. Treat the Prediction Hunt timeline as sloppy on that row. 2.2 The fee formula, precisely From Polymarket's official documentation: fee = C × feeRate × p × (1 − p) , where C is shares traded and p is the share price. For Crypto, feeRate = 0.07 — the "7%." Makers are never charged; only takers pay. Fees are computed by the protocol at match time (you don't include them in orders), collected in shares on buys and in the settlement currency on sells, and rounded to 5 decimal places (minimum charge 0.00001, so tiny extreme-price trades can round to zero). Markets carry a feesEnabled flag, and per-market parameters are queryable via getClobMarketInfo(conditionID), which returns a fee descriptor fd = {r: feeRate, e: exponent, to: takerOnly}. The presence of an exponent parameter means the curve shape itself is tunable per market — another reason to read the live values rather than hard-coding 0.07. Official crypto fee table (per 100 shares): 1¢ → $0.07; 10¢ → $0.63; 25¢ → $1.31; 50¢ → $1.75 (peak); 75¢ → $1.31; 90¢ → $0.63; 95¢ → $0.33; 99¢ → $0.07. Symmetric around 50¢. Second source conflict: the official docs table implies a 0.07 rate and a 1.75% peak, while several third-party guides (Prediction Hunt, Start Polymarket, KuCoin) report 1.80% peak / 0.072 rate — and Prediction Hunt's headline notes the effective formula reaches ~7.2% on low-probability crypto trades under what it calls the V3 formula versus V1. The likeliest explanation is the rate was adjusted at some point during 2026's repeated silent changes. Engineering answer: don't trust any static number, including this document's — pull fd.r and fd.e live per market. 2.3 Why this specific curve killed the popular strategies The p(1−p) term is the variance of a Bernoulli outcome — the fee is deliberately highest exactly where uncertainty (and HFT churn) is highest. Translating it into the numbers that matter for a bot: Entry price p Fee per share Breakeven edge needed (prob. points) Fee as % of capital deployed Fee as % of gross upside if win $0.50 1.75¢ +1.75 3.5% 3.5% $0.80 1.12¢ +1.12 1.4% 5.6% $0.95 0.33¢ +0.33 0.35% 6.65% $0.10 0.63¢ +0.63 6.3% 0.7% Read the two tails. Buying cheap shares costs ~6–7% of every dollar deployed (that's the "7% on low-probability" framing). Buying expensive near-certain shares costs ~6–7% of the gross profit you're chasing. The pre-fee last-second snipe — buy the nearly-decided side at 95–99¢ in the final seconds, collect 1–5¢ per share thousands of times — ran on margins thin enough that a fee equal to ~7% of gross upside, stacked on top of adverse selection from the occasional final-tick reversal, flipped the aggregate EV negative for most operators. Mid-range scalpers got it worse: needing a genuine +1.75-point probability edge per taker trade at 50¢, repeated hundreds of times a day, is a bar almost no signal on 5-minute BTC noise clears. And because the fee also made wash volume expensive, the reward-farming loops that inflated these books died with it. When Cointelegraph covered the January change, the X commentary it collected framed the move exactly this way — wash-trade protection and a shot at high-frequency taker bots, with the fees recycled to liquidity providers rather than kept by the platform. 3. Community Intel: Who's Actually Running Bots, and the Post-Mortems 3.1 The best loser's post-mortem found (DEV Community, March 14, 2026) A developer published a full accounting of 176 resolved trades from a Python bot on the 15-minute crypto markets, running three strategies simultaneously. The lessons map one-to-one onto the 5-minute product: Momentum win rate is a mirage at high entry prices. His momentum strategy won ~80% of the time — and lost money, because average entry was $0.85. In a binary market your win rate must exceed your entry price plus fees, so 80% at 85¢ is a structural loser. Even after capping entries at $0.72 the math stayed marginal. His summary of the core problem: when BTC has visibly moved, the UP token isn't at 50¢ anymore — it's at 90¢+, and in one logged case a clean +0.52% BTC move had UP already priced at $0.99. By the time a signal is obvious, the market has eaten the edge. Leg risk turned his best strategy into his worst losses. His both-sides arb (buy UP + DOWN when the combined price is under $1.00) had a ~97% win rate, but he used fill-or-kill orders, and occasionally one leg filled while the other was rejected — converting a $0.20 expected arb profit into a naked directional position that lost $20+. His framing: one execution failure ran 100:1 against the expected profit. Size for the failure case, not the average case. Regime filtering was the difference between 67% and 93%. Adding a chop detector (don't trade when BTC's move is a spike likely to revert rather than a trend) was, in his words, a day-one necessity he added in week two. He also cryptographically logs every decision input so 3 AM debugging has an audit trail. The bot is open source ("polymarket-ai"). 3.2 The current meta: a taxonomy of 1,000 profitable wallets (Medium, July 2026) A fresh analysis (published this week) reverse-engineered the on-chain behavior of roughly a thousand profitable Up/Down-market wallets and sorted them into six archetypes: full-set arbitrage (buy both sides under $1.00 combined); hedged directional (an arb structure deliberately tilted toward the side with modeled edge, using the other side as the hedge); repricing bots (maintain an internal fair value from the underlying spot feed and hit Polymarket quotes that lag it); last-second residual harvesters (buy the ~99¢ nearly-resolved side for the final cent — high win rate, catastrophic tail when a final-second reversal hits); plus market-making and reward-oriented variants. The single most repeated trait across the profitable population: they enter with limit orders . With edges of 1–5% per trade, taker fees plus slippage consume the entire margin, so the winners structurally position themselves as makers. This is the cleanest available evidence of what the January fee did to the ecosystem: it didn't end bot profitability, it relocated it to the passive side of the book. 3.3 Supporting data points from other operators A February 2026 analysis of six months of order book data (Q3 2025 → Q1 2026) measured the decay of the taker-arb game directly: average arbitrage opportunity lifetime fell to 2.7 seconds (from 12.3 in 2024), ~73% of arb profits were captured by sub-100ms execution, and the median arb spread compressed to ~0.3% — barely clearing costs. Its conclusion matched the wallet-taxonomy piece: the reliable strategies now are market making (it claims 0.5–2% monthly with low drawdown from backtests — treat claimed returns skeptically) and slower information-lag trades, not speed races. An April 2026 build log from a developer who wrote a BTC trading engine adds two useful mechanical facts: Polymarket has no true market order — every "market" order is a marketable limit at the best ask, which matters for how you model fills — and he ran Binance, Coinbase, and Chainlink feeds side-by-side because feed divergence visibly moved book positioning. The most-shared public reference implementation for the 5-minute game specifically (a GitHub gist, June 2026) is essentially the architecture you'd expect: sleep until T−10s, score 7 technical indicators on Binance data, fire on a confidence threshold or a sudden score spike, hard deadline at T−5s, FOK buys retried every 3 seconds until close, a GTC limit at $0.95 as fallback when the winning side has no asks, an empirical delta→token-price ladder for realistic backtests (a 0.10% BTC move implies ~$0.80 tokens; 0.15%+ implies $0.92–0.97), 5-share minimum orders, and Binance rate-limit handling. Its comment thread is where the RTDS/Chainlink strike-price confirmation in Section 1.2 comes from. Note what this bot is: a taker paying the 7% curve on every entry — the exact profile the post-mortem and wallet data say is now the hard mode. Two more artifacts worth bookmarking: polyrec (GitHub), an open-source terminal dashboard purpose-built for these markets that ingests the Chainlink oracle via RTDS, Binance 1-second klines, and the Polymarket CLOB WebSocket with 70+ indicators, CSV logging, and backtesting — a ready-made data-collection layer; and a packaged 5-minute bot "skill" on an agent marketplace (reviewed May 31, 2026) whose parameters reveal the retail meta — decide 80–100 seconds before close, require ≥85% confidence and positive EV thresholds, refuse entries above $0.85, enforce loss-streak cooldowns — and whose own limitations list includes the two structural warnings this report keeps hitting: positions effectively can't be exited as the window closes (the book empties), and the bot signals on Binance while Polymarket resolves on Chainlink. 4. Maker Rebates and Taker Rebates: Who's Earning, How 4.1 Maker Rebates Program (the liquidity-provider side) Mechanics per the official docs and help center: taker fees collected in each fee-enabled market fund a per-market rebate pool. For Crypto the rebate share is 20% of collected taker fees (most other categories: 25%; some third-party guides claim up to 50% in certain categories, which the official table contradicts — trust the docs). Distribution is daily, with a $1 minimum accrual, and is performance-based on filled maker volume : your rebate = (your fee-equivalent ÷ total fee-equivalent among makers in that market) × the market's pool, where fee-equivalent applies the same C × feeRate × p(1−p) formula to your filled maker orders. Two design consequences: you only compete against other makers in the same market, and fills near 50¢ carry the most rebate weight because that's where the fee formula peaks. Resting orders that never fill earn nothing from this program. Worked example at official rates: a taker lifts your 2,000-share offer at 50¢ ($1,000 notional). The taker pays 2,000 × 0.07 × 0.25 = $35 in fees; $7 of that (20%) goes to that market's daily maker pool, split pro-rata by fee-equivalent among everyone whose resting orders got filled that day. If you were the only maker filled, that's $7 back on a trade where you also paid zero fees and captured whatever spread you quoted. So who's earning them? Structurally: two-sided quoters in the 5m/15m books — the same HFT-style operators the fee pushed off the taker side. The profitable-wallet taxonomy (Section 3.2) and the maker-strategy guides both describe the same loop: quote both sides, collect spread + rebates, manage inventory, and accept adverse-selection risk from late-window informed flow (the residual snipers are trading against you now). An April 2026 bot-trader analysis flagged the predictable second-order effect: as rebates get richer, maker competition compresses maker profitability too. Note two adjacent programs that get confused with this one: the Liquidity Rewards Program pays daily (midnight UTC epochs, quadratic scoring, two-sided quoting favored, $1 minimum) merely for resting near the midpoint whether or not you fill — the opposite incentive — and there are also holding-reward mechanics; a May 2026 deep-dive that ranked live markets by reward structure via the Gamma API is the best current map of where those pools sit. A maker in these markets typically earns from both lanes at once but must decide which one they're optimizing, because rebates reward getting filled and rewards-farming rewards not getting filled. 4.2 Taker Rebate Program (launched May 28, 2026) This is the newest piece and it materially changes taker-bot math. Seven tiers (Bronze → Obsidian) set by rolling 30-day Weighted Volume : wV = trade size × (1 − entry price) × category weight × bonuses. Crypto carries the highest category weight, 2.3× , making 5-minute BTC volume the fastest tier ladder on the platform. Roughly $2,000 wV qualifies for the bottom tier; $10M wV reaches the top tier's 50% rebate (intermediate examples from the docs: Gold 18%, Diamond 44%). Rebates apply to fees on trades made after reaching the tier (no backfill), pay out daily at midnight UTC in pUSD (Polymarket's internal dollar token — note maker rebates are documented as paying USDC to the wallet, one of several signs the platform is mid-migration to pUSD), with a $1 minimum, plus one-time level-up bonuses from $10 (Bronze) to $25,000 (Obsidian). Only taker trades earn wV; maker fills don't count. Polymarket explicitly reserves the right to claw back rebates and tiers for wash trading and self-matching. Context for the launch: it was announced May 21–22 as an up-to-50% fee refund and read by trade press as a volume-retention move against Kalshi, which had just broken Polymarket's growth streak. Also note the wV formula's quirk: the (1 − entry price) factor means low-priced entries build tier volume disproportionately fast — a $100 taker trade at 10¢ earns nearly double the wV of the same $100 at 50¢. Net effect for a taker bot: effective crypto fee = 0.07 × p(1−p) × (1 − tier rebate). A grinder who reaches mid tiers is paying meaningfully less than the headline curve; at the top tier the 7% is effectively 3.5%. The break-even edge column in Section 2.3 scales down accordingly — but the thresholds mean this mostly rescues already-high-volume operations, not marginal ones. 5. Synthesis: What This Means for the Bot This section is my read of the evidence above rather than any single source's claim. Feed architecture. Subscribe to both RTDS topics from wss://ws-live-data.polymarket.com: crypto_prices_chainlink (btc/usd) as ground truth and strike capture, crypto_prices (btcusdt, Binance) as the leading signal. Log both tick-by-tick per window (polyrec is a ready scaffold), compute the basis, and before risking capital, measure empirically: (a) how often the final-10s Binance direction disagrees with the Chainlink settlement direction, and (b) the distribution of Chainlink tick arrival times around the boundary. Those two numbers price the exact risk that killed Binance-signaled late entries. Remember the tie rule — flat-or-up settles Up — which is a small structural thumb on the Up side worth a fraction of a cent in your fair value at near-zero deltas. Fee-aware EV gate. Every taker entry needs modeled edge ≥ 0.07 × p(1−p) × (1 − your tier rebate) + expected slippage + an adverse-selection buffer, evaluated per trade. Pull fd from getClobMarketInfo at startup and periodically — both 2026 fee changes shipped silently, and the docs changelog plus the feesEnabled/fd fields are the only reliable tripwires. Side of the book. The strongest single pattern in the post-fee data is that profitability migrated to makers. The highest-EV evolution of a directional 5-minute bot is likely hybrid: express the directional view with resting limit orders early-to-mid window (zero fee, rebate-eligible, better prices) and reserve taker crossings for high-conviction late signals that clear the full fee-plus-buffer gate. If you stay predominantly taker, the 2.3× crypto wV weight means the volume you're already doing climbs the rebate tiers faster than any other category — model your realistic 30-day wV before assuming the headline 7%. Execution hygiene from the post-mortems. Never leg into paired positions with FOK on both sides (the 100:1 half-fill loss); respect the 5-share minimum in fallback logic; assume you cannot exit in the final stretch because the book evaporates; cap entry prices so required win rate stays below realistic model accuracy (the 80%-win-rate-at-85¢ trap); add a chop/regime filter before anything else; and log every decision input, because the diagnosis of a losing week lives in the basis and timing data, not the P&L.
